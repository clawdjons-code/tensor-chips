What is a tensor chip?

A tensor chip is a specialized processor for tensor operations. A tensor is just a structured block of numbers: a scalar is 0-D, a vector is 1-D, a matrix is 2-D, and images/audio/model weights become bigger tensors.

Core idea

Lots of tiny math

AI models mostly perform repeated multiply-accumulate work: a × b + c, billions or trillions of times.

Specialization

Less general, much faster

A CPU can do nearly anything. A tensor chip does fewer things, but does the AI-shaped things brutally fast and energy-efficiently.

Where used

Training and inference

They train large models in data centers and run models on phones, laptops, cars, cameras, and servers.

Why AI wants them

Neural networks are basically enormous recipes of linear algebra plus non-linear steps. The expensive part is usually moving and multiplying numbers, not philosophizing about AGI on LinkedIn.

Parallelism

Instead of one arithmetic unit doing one operation, tensor chips use arrays of many units working at once.

Lower precision

AI often tolerates approximate numbers: FP16, BF16, INT8, FP8, sometimes lower. Smaller numbers mean faster math and less memory traffic.

Data reuse

Weights and activations are reused across many operations. Good tensor chips keep data close so they do not waste energy dragging it across the chip.

Inside a tensor chip

Compute

MAC arrays

Thousands of multiply-accumulate units arranged to process matrix tiles in parallel.

Memory

SRAM / cache

Fast on-chip memory stores tiles of weights and activations. This matters because moving data often costs more energy than computing on it.

Control

Scheduler + compiler

Software decides how to split model operations into chip-friendly chunks. Bad compilation can make expensive silicon look stupid.

Interconnect

Networking

Large training jobs need many chips talking quickly. Bandwidth and latency become survival issues.

Precision

FP/BF/INT formats

Support for FP32, TF32, FP16, BF16, FP8, INT8, etc. Different chips optimize different formats.

I/O

HBM / DRAM

High-bandwidth memory feeds the compute units. If compute waits for memory, you bought a very expensive space heater.

Names you’ll see

Google TPU

Tensor Processing Unit. Designed around matrix/tensor operations for Google-scale AI workloads.

NVIDIA Tensor Cores

Special units inside NVIDIA GPUs that accelerate matrix math, especially mixed precision AI work.

Apple Neural Engine

On-device neural processor in Apple silicon for local inference: photos, speech, ML features, and increasingly generative tasks.

NPUs

Neural Processing Units in phones and PCs. Usually optimized for efficient inference, not giant model training.

AI ASICs

Custom application-specific chips from cloud vendors and startups. Great if the workload matches. Expensive paperweight otherwise.

Edge accelerators

Small low-power chips for cameras, sensors, robotics, and embedded devices.

What they do not magically solve

Important: Tensor chips accelerate math. They do not automatically make models smarter, safer, cheaper, or easier to deploy. Silicon is not a business model. Shocking, I know.

Memory wall

If model data cannot reach compute fast enough, performance collapses.

Software stack

Compilers, kernels, drivers, frameworks, and profiling tools determine whether the chip is useful.

Model fit

Sparse, branchy, weird workloads may not map well to dense tensor arrays.

Tiny timeline

Pre-2010s
CPUs and GPUs dominate numerical computing. GPUs become useful for neural nets because they are massively parallel.
2010s
Deep learning explodes. GPUs add more dedicated matrix hardware; Google introduces TPUs for internal and cloud workloads.
2020s
AI accelerators become central infrastructure. Phones and laptops add NPUs; data centers scale GPU/TPU clusters with high-bandwidth memory and networking.

Bottom line: tensor chips are AI math engines. They are built to multiply and accumulate tensors quickly, with the right precision, memory layout, and parallelism. CPUs are generalists, GPUs are parallel beasts, tensor chips are specialized accelerators.
